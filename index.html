Introduction

“Does money buy happiness?”
The relationship between wealth and happiness has long been a topic for debate in news reports, publications, and daily conversations. Their intricate nexus is incredibly complex, so we set out to better understand the connection between the two.

No, but it can solve a lot of problems.
When considering the correlation between money and happiness, we often assume they are directly related. Having a stable income fulfills basic needs: access to food, shelter, safety, and health care. In this way, money can provide a sense of stability and control, allowing everyday mishaps to be just that, rather than a financially devastating event, i.e., an unexpected hospital bill or just taking an Uber to dodge a rainstorm.

However, wealthy people view money differently.
The ‘wealth cycle’ is just like the more widely known cycle of poverty, but inverse, involving the creation and preservation of wealth over time. After wealth is generated initially, it is preserved and grown exponentially over generations, for example, by investing in the stock market. Wealthy people and their families and trustees become more wealthy over time just by having money to begin with, continuing the cycle. This is where the injustice lies: rich people become richer, and poor people become poorer.

Wealth Distribution

Net Worth vs Liabilities

By Age: The under 40s age group has much lesser wealth, but just as much liabilities as other age brackets. If we look back over time, this is a consistent trend since the beginning of the data.

By Education: Wealth is overwhelmingly concentrated in the more well-educated population. This could be because the wealthy are able to afford higher education, leading to higher paying jobs and opportunities, perpetuating the pattern. In fact, wealth disparity has increased, from 50% of the total net worth in the US belonging to the college-educated in the early 1990s, to 70% now. Liabilities are somewhat similar, but with a higher proportion for the uneducated. Liabilities have not grown at the same rate.

By top% net worth: The top 10% has 70% of total wealth in the US, 28 times the bottom 50%. The top 1% has more than ten times the bottom 50%, over 30% of the total, and of that, the top 0.1% has more than five times that of the bottom 50%, over 12% of total. Liabilities are distributed proportionally.

By race: The majority of US assets belong to the white population, though it has decreased from 90% to 80%, primarily from "Other" growth threefold from 3-9% in the 2000s. Liabilities are generally proportional. For reference, the U.S. Census states the population as 60% white, 13% Black, and 18% Hispanic.

It is important to note that the differences between net income and net worth charts reflect the difference in generational money. The net income graph is therefore not as heavily skewed as net worth. Next, we will discuss how wealth inequality is perpetuated.

Case Study: COVID-19 Pandemic

We can see that billionaire net worth has been increasing rapidly over the past decades, but during the pandemic, their worth increased drastically. The wealthy are comfortable even in times of financial crisis, and can leverage their wealth while the average person has no choice but to use it to survive. For example, when stimulus checks were distributed, if you were living paycheck-to-paycheck, you would need that money to fulfill basic needs. However, if you didn't need the money immediately, and invested it, you could've seen a return of thousands of percent.

Billionaires’ wealth has risen more since COVID-19 began than it has in the last decade, by at least $5 trillion dollars. This surge in wealth is undeniable, for many reasons. One could be central banks putting trillions of dollars into markets to bolster the economy, with much of that effort ending up in the pockets of billionaires riding the stock market boom. Another consideration could be vaccine costs: vaccines were meant to end the pandemic, but some governments allowed pharma billionaires to monopolize on vaccines, cutting off supplies for billions of people. Such extreme inequality is a form of economic violence, where people with more wealth could take part in the policies and political decisions to perpetuate the wealth of the privileged few.

There is no denying the extreme inequality we face based on the above analysis.

The Gini Coefficient

Hover over a state to see the Gini coefficient for the selected year or press play to watch income inequality change over time.

How is wealth inequality quantified? One measure, the Gini coefficient, measures income distribution across a population, and can serve as a decent gauge of economic inequality. A higher coefficient indicates greater inequality, on a scale from 0 to 1 - so a 1 would represent complete inequality, and a 0 would represent complete equality. Globally, this index has increased over the past few centuries, and has grown rapidly during the COVID-19 pandemic. This is demonstrated clearly in the above map, showing Gini coefficient data for each state from 2006 to 2021. From 2019 to 2021, a significant portion of the map is a much deeper blue, indicating a large average Gini coefficient, and consequently a larger inequality in income. As explained before, the pandemic showed the extremes to which the rich can grow richer, especially the top one percent. Overall, income inequality has only increased over time, with the highest state Gini coefficient increasing from 0.5 to 0.514, and the United States total Gini index increasing from 0.48 to 0.5.

Income Inequality and Happiness

For the purpose of our research, we decided to focus on mental health as our indicator of happiness, specifically depression rates. Typically, one would expect greater income inequality to correlate with greater rates of depression. Inequality can negatively impact social cohesion and capital, and increase chronic stress, potentially placing individuals at a heightened risk of depression. In the table below, however, no such correlation can be found. In fact, the countries with the greatest rates of depression were also countries with a low Gini coefficient (inequality). One possible explanation for this is that countries with higher income inequality have lower rates of self-attestation to depression. In Zimbabwe, one of the countries with the lowest depression rates and highest income inequality, they call the most common mental illness “kufungisisa”, which means ‘excessive worry about a problem’ in Shona. Many say that kufungisisa is not an illness, but a reaction to the stresses of life, such as poverty or disease. Depression is not categorized in the same way in Zimbabwe as it is in the West, where many might determine kufungisisa as such. As a result, the official count for depressive disorders may not be as high as the true count, and this is likely to apply to other countries with high income inequality. On the flip side, developed countries such as Greece and Portugal are much more likely to have an official count close to their real count.

Conclusion

Overall, it appears that wealth does not necessarily correlate with happiness in the typical sense, but it is clear that the fulfillment of basic needs is an essential before happiness can be considered. We focused on the United States for most of our research, as it is most relevant for our purposes, and wealth inequality is only increasing here. People with generational wealth have access to more resources, and thus are able to continue to build wealth, while those without must prioritize basic needs over wealth accumulation. While income inequality is not the final determinant of wealth, it is accurate enough to show, especially during the pandemic, that this issue can contribute to overall happiness. However, we saw that many happiness metrics, including mental health data, can fail to show this correlation, given different understandings of happiness. A 2020 study from Princeton University showed that money could make you happy up to an income of $75,000 - while this still could be true, it reveals the true issue: not money itself, but the disparity of wealth involved.
