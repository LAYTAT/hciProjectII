Introduction

“Does money buy you happiness?”

This is where the injustice lies, and rich people might be able to make more money because they have more money.

Wealth inequality

Net Worth vs Liabilities

By Age: The under 40s age group has much lesser wealth, but just as much liabilities as other agr brackets. If we look back in time, this is a consistent trend since the beginning of the data.

By Education: Wealth is overwhelmingly concentrated in the more well educated population. This could be because the more wealthy are able to afford higher education, leading to higher paying jobs and opportunities, perpetuating the pattern. In fact, the wealth disparity has increased, from 50% of total net worth in the US belonging to then college educated in the early 1990s to 70% now. Liabilities are somewhat the same, but with higher proportion for uneducated. Liabilities have not grown at the same rate.

By top% net worth: The top 10% has 70% of total wealth in the US, 28x the bottom 50%. The op 1% has has more than 10x bottom 50%, over 30% of the total, and of that, the top 0.1% has more than 5x that of the bottom 50%, over 12% of total. Liabilities are distributed proportionally.

By race: The majority of US assets belong to the white population, though it has decreased from 90% to 80%, primarily from "Other" growth threefold from 3-9% in the 2000s. Liabilities are generally proportional. For reference, the US Census says the population is white 60%, black 13%, and hispanic 18%.

We can note that the differences between net income and net worth charts reflect the difference in generational money. The net income graph is therefore not as heavily skewed as net worth. Now we will cover how this wealth inequality perpetuates.

Explanation of gini coefficient meaning and calculation

One would expect greater income inequality to correlate with greater rates of depression. Inequality can negatively impact social cohesion and capital, and increase chronic stress, potentially placing individuals at a heightened risk of depression. In observing the below table, however, no such correlation can be found. In fact, the countries with the greatest rates of depression were also countries with a low gini coefficient (inequality). One possible explanation for this is that countries with higher income inequality have lower rates of self-attestation to depression. In Zimbabwe, one of the countries with the lowest depression rates and highest income inequality, they call the most common mental illness “kufungisisa”, which means excessive worry about a problem in Shona. Many say that kufungisisa is not an illness, but a reaction to the stresses of life, such as poverty or disease. Depression is not boxed in the same way in Zimbabwe as it is in the West, where many in may kufungisisa as such. As a result, the official count for depressive disorders may not be as high as the real count, and this is likely to apply to other countries with high income inequality. On the flip side, developed countries such as Greece and Portugal are much more likely to have an official count close to their real count.
